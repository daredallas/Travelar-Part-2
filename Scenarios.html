Scenarios

Scenario 1: Abcde wants quick information about restaurants

Abcde is on a business trip to Tokyo. She has a bit of downtime between closing deals. Outside the downtown building her client is based out of she finds herself overwhelmed by the restaurant options around her and can’t read the signs. With only thirty minutes before her final meeting before heading home, she wants to make her last Japanese meal special. She starts the app and points her phone at the restaurants around her, for each location the app shows her the names of the restaurant, summary tags, a user rating, and an option with link to a menu.

Implementation result: It is clear to see that after Abcde logs into the app, she will see an AR camera view. She can point her phone at restaurants around her and from the “name bubbles” she can tap on the bubbles and the app will show her names, rating, description. Once she clicks on “more” she can also see the menu and the tags.

Scenario 2: Karen has a bad experience and wants to leave a review

Karen and her cousin Barbara are on a rare trip to Miami to enjoy the beach. A travel agency has organized the whole trip for them, including the hotel they are staying at. The agency also recommended the app to her and she was interested but confused by the concept. On the second morning of their trip, they visit the hotels adjacent restaurant before heading out to the beach. Left upset with the overpriced food, slow service, and dirty utensils, Karen remembers the travel agent told her to point the app at any business she wanted to interact with. She takes out her phone, points it at the restaurant, and is pleased when she sees an option to leave a review.

Implementation result: When Karen logs in she can point at the restaurant, click on the bubble, click on more and she will see the “Review” button. She can also click on the stars and it will take her directly to the reviews page, where she will see other users’ reviews as well as a “review” button.

Scenario 3: Gunther needs an alternative

Gunther is getting sponsored by Lufthansa airlines to test out their new first-class experience with a free flight and free stay in a partner hotel. Feeling like his meal should match the rest of his experience, and make great content for his blog, he plans to have a fine dining experience. Upon his arrival at the location which his hotel concierge recommended to him, Gunther discovers that reservations are required. Not about to miss out on an experience for his blog, he points his iPad at the restaurant, clicks on the fine dining tag, and is shown a list of nearby restaurants with the same tag. He selects one with a high rating and the app shows a simple list of directions and he begins to travel there.

Implementation result: Gunther wants to find restaurants similar to his initial choice. He logs in and points to the restaurant he was initially interested in, clicks on the bubble, and then presses ‘More’. At the bottom of the more description, he will find tags associated with this location. Gunther taps on the ‘fine dining’ tag and is given a list of alternative destinations to pick from that are within walking distance (distance clearly visible next to the restaurant name), sorted according to the reviews. He pics the highest rated one and is directed there.

Scenario 4: Bobby wants more information

Bobby’s parents have taken him on a trip to France with a recent tax refund. On his week-long vacation, Bobby has been a bit bored by the museums and is eager for something more exciting. Before their visit to Disneyland Paris, Bobby’s dad Sean insisted they stop to check out a historic church on the way. Bobby can’t wait to leave, and with no English information posted anywhere nearby the stop is turning out to be longer than expected. Playing around on his phone, Bobby remembers and opens the app then begins to look around with it. Seeing information about the church’s name, a short description, and a link to the website pop up, Bobby quickly shows it to his dad. Satisfied by the concise information on the app, Sean moves the family vacation along.

Implementation result: Bobby wants to show his parents concise information about an attraction. He fires up the app and points at the church and taps the bubble overlayed on the church. The hovercard displays brief information about the site. The family is now able of moving onto more exciting activities. If they decide they want to know more they can tap on the "more" button where they find hours, website and contact information as well as a longer description.
